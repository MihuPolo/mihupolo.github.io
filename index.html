Introduction to Module

Unit 1: An Introduction to Security and Risk Management

Unit 1 offers a comprehensive introduction to foundational concepts in security and risk management. It discusses various expert definitions of risk, underscoring its complexity and context-dependent nature of risks. Key concepts include risk assessment, qualification, and mitigation using both qualitative and quantitative methods. The unit highlights the core principles of information security: confidentiality, integrity, and availability. The unit explores common risk standards, threat modeling, and risk assessment processes. It highlights the importance of integrating risk management systems with organizational processes and the PDCA (Plan, Do, Check, Act) cycle for continuous improvement. Practical aspects of risk management are addressed through case scenarios, focusing on various mitigation mechanisms, including physical, procedural, and technical controls.

Unit 2: Threats and Vulnerabilities

Unit 2 dives into the relationship between threats and vulnerabilities. According to NIST CSRC, a vulnerability is a technical weakness exploitable by a threat source, while a threat is a potential adverse event impacting organizational operations. The unit introduces the Threat Modelling Manifesto, emphasizing systematic approaches, informed creativity, varied viewpoints, useful toolkits, and theory into practice. It also discusses anti-patterns to avoid, such as over-focusing and the "hero" mentality. Key threat management models covered include STRIDE & DREAD, CVSS, OCTAVE, Attack Trees, PASTA, MITRE ATT&CK, and OWASP. The unit underscores the importance of early threat modeling in the development cycle and using varied tools and methodologies to effectively address and mitigate security risks.

Unit 3: Security and Risk Management Standards

Unit 3 provides a comprehensive overview of critical security and risk management standards essential for organizations. It covers key frameworks such as GDPR, ISO 27001, PCI-DSS, and NIST, detailing their principles, objectives, and implementation strategies. The comparison between COBIT and ITIL offers insights into governance and operational approaches, highlighting their roles in ensuring security and efficiency. Overall, the unit underscores the importance of integrating these standards to achieve robust cybersecurity, compliance, and continual improvement in organizational resilience.

Unit 4: Quantitative Risk Modelling

Quantitative Risk Modelling, explores quantitative risk modeling methods, contrasting them with traditional qualitative approaches like risk matrices. It covers three main techniques: Monte Carlo Simulation: This method uses random sampling to model complex systems' outcomes. Key points include defining input domains, selecting probability distributions, and ensuring accurate pseudo-random number generation. Principles stress distribution justification and result analysis, while anti-patterns caution against tool sensitivity and mismatched distributions. Bayes Theorem: A statistical method for updating beliefs based on new evidence, involving calculating posterior probabilities from prior beliefs and likelihoods. Named after Rev. Thomas Bayes, it emphasizes subjective probabilities and the impact of hypothesis/prior selection. Anti-patterns highlight subjective interpretation and the influence of prior information. Multi-Criteria Decision Analysis (MCDA): Aids decision-making across multiple criteria, categorized into choice, sorting, ranking, description, elimination, and design problems. Techniques like TOPSIS, AHP, and ANP are chosen based on the decision type. Principles focus on selecting suitable techniques and preparing data accurately, while anti-patterns warn against incorrect technique selection and resulting errors in output. Unit 4 underscores the shift towards quantitative methods for precise risk assessment and management, emphasizing their practical applications and considerations.

Unit 5: Business Continuity and Disaster Recovery

Business Continuity and Disaster Recovery: discusses business continuity (BC) and disaster recovery (DR), which are closely intertwined, with DR typically considered a subset of BC. The importance of BC plans was highlighted during the 2020 pandemic, as businesses had to adapt to government-mandated shutdowns by enabling remote work securely. This shift introduced new security risks, necessitating updated policies and secure IT measures at home. BC, as defined in ISO 22301, ensures business operations continue during crises through risk assessments, business impact analyses (BIA), and robust policies. Regular testing ensures readiness. DR focuses on IT infrastructure availability during BC activation, driven by Recovery Time Objectives (RTO) and Recovery Point Objectives (RPO) derived from BIA. Digitalization has intensified BC and DR needs, with cloud-based DR as a Service (DRaaS) offering flexible and cost-effective solutions. Cloud-native applications align with modern DR strategies, leveraging scalability and resilience. Architectural decisions like single vs. multi-cloud providers and hybrid cloud models impact DR effectiveness and cost. Key considerations include data redundancy, availability zones, and platform resilience when selecting cloud-based DR solutions. Technologies like Azure's Availability Sets and Always On Availability Groups enhance database continuity. Understanding these elements ensures robust BC and DR frameworks aligned with business needs and digital strategies.

Unit 6: An Introduction to Risk Models and Approaches

Unit 6: An Introduction to Risk Models and Approaches: explores diverse risk models and approaches crucial to modern security and risk management. Foundational methods like Monte Carlo simulations and Bayesian theory are highlighted for quantifying risks. Specialized models such as Real Options Theory and the Options Pricing Theory (Black-Scholes Model) are discussed, showcasing their applications from business decisions to environmental management. Enterprise Risk Management (ERM) is emphasized for its role in integrating risk across organizational portfolios, promoting resilience and shareholder value. The evolution to DevSecOps underscores the need for continuous security integration in software development, addressing vulnerabilities early. The unit also explores the intersection of Machine Learning (ML) with cybersecurity and the challenges of adversarial ML, emphasizing the need for robust defense strategies. Insights from the NSA's Science of Security initiative outline critical cybersecurity challenges and advanced scientific approaches.

Assinment

During this course our first assignment has been a team project, to create a Risk Identification Report of Pampered Pets Business. Our team, consisting of Olimpia W., Craig N., and myself, collaborated on a risk identification report for Pampered Pets Business. We used WhatsApp, email, and university SharePoint for communication and document sharing. Initially, we signed a team group contract and decided to assign roles after some collaboration. Craig N., with his experience in writing cybersecurity reports, eventually took the lead, guiding us through the report plan and task distribution.

The assessor noted mentioned that the report demonstrates a thorough understanding of ISO 31000, STRIDE, and DREAD methodologies, emphasizing their relevance to Pampered Pets. It identifies risks for both the current and potential digitalized states of the business. The critical analysis effectively justifies the combination of STRIDE and DREAD methodologies but could benefit from a detailed comparison of the current and post-digitalization risk landscapes. To further enhance the report, we recommend explicitly discussing the suitability of these methodologies and providing real-world examples. From my perspective, I enjoyed working in a team. It has its charm, and you always know that someone will cover for you in case of failure. The collaborative effort in preparing this report allowed us to leverage diverse perspectives and expertise, leading to a more comprehensive analysis. Team discussions often brought up points that I might have overlooked if working alone, and the mutual support kept the project moving smoothly, even when individual challenges arose.

The second assignment, I analysed the transition to digitalization by integration an international supply chain alongside automated warehouses, the integration introduces both quality and supply chain risks. Utilizing Monte Carlo Simulation, we identified that the combined probability of quality risks is approximately 37.15%, while supply chain risks stand at around 40.65%. To mitigate these risks, we recommend implementing robust quality control measures, enhancing cybersecurity, and optimizing supply chain processes. For disaster recovery, a high-availability architecture with cloud-based replication, automated failover, and a multi-cloud strategy ensures minimal data loss and continuous availability. By adopting these strategies, the company can safeguard product quality and supply chain security amid digital transformation.

Step-by-Step Guide to Creating a Debian OS Cybersecurity Workstation

Building a work machine for practicing cybersecurity skills on a Debian-based system is an excellent choice. Debian is a stable, versatile, and widely-used Linux distribution that provides a strong foundation for learning and deploying cybersecurity tools. Here’s how you can evolve this thought and build a comprehensive cybersecurity practice environment.

Because Debian OS is free to use and it is quite difficult to find a PC with preinstalled Linux, especially Debian OS, as a first move, I am going to describe step by step the process of installing the system.

Step-by-Step Guide to Installing Debian OS:

1. Download Debian ISO:

- Go to the Debian official website (Debian.org) and download the latest stable release ISO file.
- Choose the appropriate ISO file based on your system architecture (e.g., amd64 for 64-bit systems).

2. Create a Bootable USB Drive:

- As I have been doing that on Windows OS, I’ve always use Rufus to create a bootable USB drive.
- Download and open Rufus from rufus.ie.
- Select your USB drive and the Debian ISO file.
- Click Start and wait for the process to complete.

3. Boot from the USB Drive:

- Insert the bootable USB drive into your PC.
- Restart your PC and enter the BIOS/UEFI setup by pressing a key (often F2, F12, DEL, or ESC – depends
of your computer brand) during startup.
- Change the boot order to boot from the USB drive first.
- Save the changes and exit the BIOS/UEFI setup.

4. Start the Debian Installer:

- After booting from the USB drive, you’ll see the Debian Installer menu.
- Select Graphical install for an easy-to-use graphical installation process.

5. Select Language and Locale Settings:

- Choose your preferred language.
- Select your location to set the time zone and system locale.
- Configure your keyboard layout.

6. Configure the Network:

- If you have a wired connection, it should automatically detect and configure it.
- For wireless connections, select your network and enter the necessary credentials.

7. Set Up Users and Passwords:

- Set a strong password for the root account (administrator privileges).
- Create a new user account for everyday use. Enter the full name, username, and password for this account.

8. Partition Disks:

- Choose a partitioning method. For beginners, the Guided - use entire disk option is recommended.
- Select the disk to partition.
- Choose how to partition the disk (all files in one partition is the simplest option).
- Review and confirm the partitioning setup. Be aware that this will erase all data on the selected disk.

9. Install the Base System, this process may take a few minutes.

10. Configure the Package Manager:

- Choose whether to scan another CD/DVD. If you downloaded additional packages, you can scan them now,
but typically you’ll select No.
- Configure the package manager by selecting a network mirror close to your location for downloading
software packages.
- Decide whether to participate in the package usage survey (optional).

11. Install Software and Choose Desktop Environment:

- Select software to install. For a standard desktop environment, select Debian desktop environment and
your preferred desktop environment (e.g., GNOME, KDE, XFCE, etc.).
- You can also select additional utilities like SSH server or standard system utilities.

12. Install the GRUB Bootloader:

- When prompted, choose to install the GRUB bootloader to the primary drive. This is necessary for booting
your system.
- Confirm the device for boot loader installation (usually the main disk).

13. Once the installation is complete, the installer will prompt you to remove the installation media and restart the system.

14. First Boot and Post-Installation Steps:

- Remove the USB drive and reboot your PC.
- Log in with the user account you created during the installation.
- Update the system to ensure all packages are up to date.

15. Update the system through terminal by writing: sudo apt update && sudo apt upgrade -y

Conclusion

By following these steps, you can successfully install Debian OS on your PC. This installation provides a solid foundation for further configuring your machine for cybersecurity practice or any other use case you have in mind.
